\section{Introduction}
In estmation theory there is a great importance in evaluating the prediction
error of the model. In the majority of cases cross-validation suffices. However,
when the dataset is "small" cross-validation estimates this error poorly, in
this case the partition of the validation set becomes a deciding factor of the
error estimate. A way to combat this is to look at covariance penalties which
offers a more deterministic approach to the errro estimate with the cost of
having additional assumptions.

If we focus specifically on trees we can show some interesting? findings...

\section{Independent copy construction}
Consider iid trainingsdata $\left((X_i, Y_i)\right)_{i=1}^n$ and independent test response data $Y^*$. Starting of with the general expression for prediction error

\begin{align}
	\textbf{MSEP} & = \E \left( (Y^* - \widehat\mu)^2 \right),
	\label{msep}
\end{align}
where the $ * $ denots the data being an independent copy of the trainingsdata
used to create the estimator $ \widehat\mu $. Using this general expression for
prediction error we can manipulate it in the following way
\begin{align}
	\E \left( (Y^* - \widehat\mu)^2 \right) & = \E \left( (Y^* - \mu + \mu -
	\widehat\mu)^2 \right)                                                        \\
	                                        & = \E \left( (Y^* - \mu)^2 \right) +
	2\E \left( (Y^* - \mu)(\widehat \mu - \mu) \right)  +
	\E \left( (\widehat\mu - \mu)^2 \right),
\end{align}
where $ \mu (x) = \E[Y \mid X =x] $ is the true regression function. Since we assume that $ Y^* $ and $ \widehat\mu $ are independent the second term
in the above expansion is identically zero. Furthermore, since $ \widehat\mu =
	\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n} Y_j$, we can express the prediction error as
\begin{align}
	\textbf{MSEP} & = \E \left( (Y^* - \mu)^2 \right) +
	2\E \left( (Y^* - \mu)(\widehat \mu - \mu) \right)  +
	\E \left( (\widehat\mu - \mu)^2 \right)                   \\
	              & = \E \left( (Y^* - \mu)^2 \right) +
	\E \left( (\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n} Y_j - \mu)^2 \right) \\
	              & = \sigma^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n},
	\label{pred_err}
\end{align}
where $\sigma^2 := \E(\sigma^2(X))$ (in the heteroscedastic case). One can interepret this result as the prediction error on a specific leaf of a
tree. In the most simple case one can see this as the prediction error when
there is no split.

Using simple probability one can once again manipulate
expression (\ref{msep}).
\begin{align}
	\textbf{MSEP}_{split} & = \E \left( (Y^* - \widehat\mu)^2 \right)        \\
	                      & = \E \left( (Y^* - \widehat\mu)^2 \right | X \in
	\mathcal{A})\P(X \in \mathcal{A})                                        \\
	                      & + \E \left( (Y^* - \widehat\mu)^2 \right | X \in
	\mathcal{A}^c)\P(X \in \mathcal{A}^c).
\end{align}
Performing similar analysis as done in (\ref{pred_err}), we get the expression
\begin{align}
	\textbf{MSEP}_{split} & = \sigma^2_{\mathcal{A}}( 1 + \frac{1}{n_{\mathcal{A}} })\pi_{\mathcal{A}} +
	\sigma^2_{\mathcal{A}^c}(1 + \frac{1}{n_{\mathcal{A}^c}})\pi_{\mathcal{A}^c},
\end{align}
where
$$
	\sigma^2_{\cdot} := \Var\left(Y \mid X \in \cdot \right),
$$
$\pi_{\cdot}, n_{\cdot}$ is the
variance, probability and number of samples respectively. An interpretation of
this is having a binary split of the data.

A natural question when building tree models is, "should a split be made on this
node?". One way to make a decision about this is to compare if the prediction
error when making the split is lower than the non-split error, that is
\begin{align}
	\textbf{MSEP}_{split} > \textbf{MSEP}.
	\label{split_criterion}
\end{align}
If the inequality holds true then a split should \textbf{not} be made, since
doing so will increase the prediction error. The rule in (\ref{split_criterion})
can be written as
\begin{align}\label{eq:Stop}
	\sigma^2_{\mathcal{A}}( 1 + \frac{1}{n_{\mathcal{A}} })\pi_{\mathcal{A}} +
	\sigma^2_{\mathcal{A}^c}(1 + \frac{1}{n_{\mathcal{A}^c}})\pi_{\mathcal{A}^c} >
	\sigma^2(1 + \frac{1}{n}).
\end{align}
Note that $ n = n_{\mathcal{A}} + n_{\mathcal{A}^c} $.

\section{Insample construction}
In this case we will look at a construction which is based on the insample
error. We assume homoscedacity, i.e. $\Var(Y \mid X) = \sigma^2$ is non-random.

Fix $x_1, \dots, x_N$ and let $\mathcal{L}(Y_i) = \mathcal{L}(Y^*_i) = \mathcal{L}\left(Y \mid X = x_i \right)$, with indpendence between $Y^*_i$ and $Y_i$, $i =1, \dots, n$ . Let $\hat \mu (x) = \hat \mu(x, (x_i, Y_i)_{i=1}^N)$ be an estimate of $\mu$ depending on the trainingsdata. Following Olas lecture notes,
$$
	\MSEPin = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (Y_i^* - \hat \mu(x_i))^2, \quad \MSE = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (Y_i - \hat \mu(x_i))^2,
$$
and we get that
\begin{align*}
	  & \E[\MSEPin] - \E[\MSE]                                                                                                                                                                                           \\
	= & \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \E \left[(Y_i^* - \hat \mu(x_i))^2 - (Y_i - \hat \mu(x_i))^2 \right]                                                                                                                    \\
	= & \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \E [(Y_i^*)^2] - \E [(Y_i)^2] + \E [\hat \mu(x_i)^2] - \E [\hat \mu(x_i)^2] + 2  \left(\E\left[ Y_i \hat \mu(x_i) \right] -  \E\left[ Y_i^* \hat \mu(x_i) \right]\right) \right) \\
	= & \frac{2}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left(  \E\left[ Y_i \hat \mu(x_i) \right] -  \E\left[ Y_i \right] \E \left[ \hat \mu(x_i) \right]\right)
	= \frac{2}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \Cov(Y_i, \hat \mu(x_i)).
\end{align*}
In the second-last equality, we have used the independence between $Y_i^*$ and $\hat \mu(x_i)$ and the fact that $\E[Y_i^*] = \E[Y_i]$. Hence,
$$
	\E[\MSEPin] = \E[\MSE] +  \frac{2}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \Cov(Y_i, \hat \mu(x_i)).
$$

\section{Distribution of stopping variable}

Consider $n = n_A + n_B$ datapoints $(x_i, Y_i)_{i=1}^n$, $\mathcal{L}(Y_i) =  \mathcal{L}(Y \mid X = x_i) = \text{Normal}(\mu(x_i), \sigma^2)$. In other words, writing $\mu_i = \mu(x_i)$, $(Y_i)$ follows a multivariate normal distribution with mean
$$
	\mu = (\mu_1, \dots, \mu_n)^T, \quad \Sigma = \sigma^2 I_n.
$$
Consider two disjoint sets $A, B$ with $x_1, \dots, x_{n_A} \in A$ and \\ $x_{n_A +1}, \dots, x_{n} \in B$ and let $C := A \dot \cup B$. Further, set
$$
	\sigma^2_A = \Var(Y \mid X \in A) = \E[(Y - \E[Y \mid X \in A])^2 \mid X \in A]
$$
Note that in general $\sigma^2 \leq \sigma^2_A$. Define $\sigma^2_B$ and $\sigma^2_C$ likewise. Estimating $\pi_A$ by $n_A/n$ and $\sigma^2_A$ by the unbiased variance estimator
$$
	\hat \sigma^2_A = \frac{1}{n_A - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} (Y_i - \bar Y_A)^2,
$$
where $\bar Y_A = 1/n_A \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} Y_i$, and analogously for the sets $B, C$, and plugging this into the above motivated $\eqref{eq:Stop}$, we arrive at the stopping criterion
\begin{align}\label{eq:Stop2}
	 & Z :=                                                                                                                             \\
	 & \frac{n_A + 1}{n_A - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} (Y_i - \bar Y_A)^2 + \frac{n_B + 1}{n_B - 1} \sum_{i= n_A + 1}^{n} (Y_i - \bar Y_B)^2 -
	\frac{n+1}{n - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (Y_i - \bar Y)^2 > 0.
\end{align}
Let
\begin{align}
	D_A =\left[\begin{array}{@{}c|c@{}}
			           \begin{matrix}
				1 & \cdots & 1 \\
				  & \vdots     \\
				1 & \cdots & 1
			\end{matrix}
			                    & \quad \bigzero \quad \\
			           \hline                          \\
			           \bigzero &
			           \bigzero                        \\
			           \quad
		           \end{array}\right],
	\quad
	I_A =\left[\begin{array}{@{}c|c@{}}
			           \quad                           \\
			           \quad \bigone_{n_A} \quad
			                    & \quad \bigzero \quad \\
			           \quad                           \\
			           \hline                          \\
			           \bigzero &
			           \bigzero                        \\
			           \quad
		           \end{array}\right]
\end{align}
and $D_B, I_B$ defined correspondingly, with the one-matrix and the identity matrix in the lower right block instead. We can then rewrite \eqref{eq:Stop2} as a quadratic form
\begin{align*}
	Z = Y^T Q Y,
\end{align*}
where
\begin{align*}
	Q = & \frac{n_A +1}{n_A -1} \left(I_A - \frac{1}{n_A} D_A\right)^T\left(I_A - \frac{1}{n_A} D_A\right) +       \\
	    & \frac{n_B +1}{n_B -1} \left(I_B - \frac{1}{n_B} D_B\right)^T\left(I_B - \frac{1}{n_B} D_B\right) -       \\
	    & \frac{n +1}{n -1} \left(I_n - \frac{1}{n} \mathbf{1} \right)^T\left(I_n - \frac{1}{n} \mathbf{1}\right).
\end{align*}
Here, $\mathbf{1}$ is the matrix of ones only. Using that all matrices in brackets are symmetric and idempotent,
\begin{align*}
	 & Q = \frac{n_A +1}{n_A -1} \left(I_A - \frac{1}{n_A} D_A\right) +
	\frac{n_B +1}{n_B -1}\left(I_B - \frac{1}{n_B} D_B\right) -
	\frac{n +1}{n -1}\left(I_n - \frac{1}{n} \mathbf{1}\right) =        \\
	 &
	\left[\begin{array}{@{}c|c@{}}
			      \begin{matrix}
				\frac{n_A +1}{n_A} - \frac{n+1}{n}              & \frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} - \frac{n_A +1}{(n_A -1)n_A} & \cdots \\
				\frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} - \frac{n_A +1}{(n_A -1)n_A} &                                                          \\
				\vdots                                          & \ddots                                          &
			\end{matrix}
			                                       & \begin{matrix}
				                                   \frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} & \cdots & \\
				                                   \vdots                        \\
			                                   \end{matrix}                                           \\
			      \hline                                                                                                     \\
			      \begin{matrix}
				\frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} & \cdots & \\
				\vdots                        \\
			\end{matrix} &
			      \begin{matrix}
				\frac{n_B +1}{n_B} - \frac{n+1}{n}              & \frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} - \frac{n_B +1}{(n_B -1)n_B} & \cdots \\
				\frac{n+1}{(n-1)n} - \frac{n_B +1}{(n_B -1)n_B} &                                                          \\
				\vdots                                          & \ddots                                          &
			\end{matrix} \\
		      \end{array}\right].
\end{align*}
Q has four distinct eigenvalues
\begin{align*}
	\lambda_A =            & \frac{n_A +1}{n_A -1} - \frac{n+1}{n-1} = \frac{2 n_B}{(n_A -1)(n-1)} > 0, \\
	\lambda_B =            & \frac{n_B +1}{n_B -1} - \frac{n+1}{n-1} = \frac{2 n_A}{(n_B -1)(n-1)} > 0, \\
	\lambda_{\text{neg}} = & -\frac{n+1}{n-1} < 0,                                                      \\
	\lambda_0 =            & 0
\end{align*}
with corresponding eigenspaces
\begin{align}
	\begin{split}\label{eq:Eigenspaces}
		\text{Eig}_{\lambda_A} = &\text{span}\left(
		\begin{bmatrix}
				1      \\
				-1     \\
				0      \\
				\vdots \\
				0      \\
				\mathbf{0}_{n_B}
			\end{bmatrix},
		\dots,
		\begin{bmatrix}
				1      \\
				0      \\
				\vdots \\
				0      \\
				-1     \\
				\mathbf{0}_{n_B}
			\end{bmatrix}
		\right),
		\quad
		\text{Eig}_{\lambda_B} = \text{span}\left(
		\begin{bmatrix}
				\mathbf{0}_{n_A} \\
				1                \\
				-1               \\
				0                \\
				\vdots           \\
				0
			\end{bmatrix},
		\dots,
		\begin{bmatrix}
				\mathbf{0}_{n_{A}} \\
				1                  \\
				0                  \\
				\vdots             \\
				0                  \\
				-1
			\end{bmatrix}
		\right),  \\
		\text{Eig}_{\lambda_{\text{neg}}} = &\text{span}\left(
		\begin{bmatrix}
				n_B    \\
				\vdots \\
				n_B    \\
				-n_A   \\
				\vdots \\
				-n_A
			\end{bmatrix}
		\right), \quad
		\text{Eig}_{\lambda_0} = \text{span}\left(
		\begin{bmatrix}
				1      \\
				\vdots \\
				1
			\end{bmatrix}
		\right)
	\end{split}
\end{align}
and hence geometric multiplicities $n_A -1, n_B -1, 1$ and $1$, respectively. Let
$$
	P = \left[\begin{array}{@{}c|c|c|c@{}}
			P_{11}
			         & \bigzero
			         & \begin{matrix}
				           n_B/\sqrt{n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2} \\
				           \vdots                           \\
				           n_B/\sqrt{n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2}
			           \end{matrix}
			         &
			\begin{matrix}
				1/\sqrt{n} \\
				\vdots     \\
				1/\sqrt{n}
			\end{matrix}                                \\
			\hline
			\bigzero &
			P_{22}
			         & \begin{matrix}
				           -n_A/\sqrt{n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2} \\
				           \vdots                            \\
				           -n_A/\sqrt{n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2}
			           \end{matrix}
			         & \begin{matrix}
				           1/\sqrt{n} \\
				           \vdots     \\
				           1/\sqrt{n}
			           \end{matrix}
		\end{array}\right],
$$
where $P_{11}$ ($P_{22}$) is an $n_A \times n_A -1$ ($n_B \times n_B -1$) matrix obtained by applying the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization to the first $n_A$ (last $n_B$) components of the vectors spanning $\text{Eig}_{\lambda_A}$ ($\text{Eig}_{\lambda_B}$) from \eqref{eq:Eigenspaces} above.

By Mathai, Provost, "Quadratic forms in random variables", page 29, formula (3.1a.4), it follows that
\begin{align}
	\begin{split}\label{eq:Z_law}
		Z = &Y^T Q Y \\
		\eqdis &\frac{\sigma^2}{n-1} \left(
		\frac{2 n_B}{n_A -1} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A - 1} (U_i + b_i)^2
		+ \frac{2 n_A}{n_B -1} \sum_{i= n_A}^{n -2} (U_i + b_i)^2
		- (n+1) (U_{n-1} + b_{n-1})^2
		\right).
		%\\
		%\eqdis &\frac{\sigma^2}{n-1} \left( 
		%\frac{2 n_B}{n_A -1} \chi^2_A(n_A -1, \xi_A)
		%+ \frac{2 n_A}{n_B -1} \chi^2_B(n_B -1, \xi_B)
		%- (n+1) \chi^2(1, b_{n-1}^2)
		%\right)
	\end{split}
\end{align}
Here, $U_1, \dots, U_{n-1}$ are iid standard normally distributed and, with $\mu^T = (\mu_A^T, \mu_B^T)$,
$$
	b = \frac{1}{\sigma}P^T \mu =
	\frac{1}{\sigma}\begin{bmatrix}
		P_{11}^T \mu_A                                                                                      \\
		P_{22}^T \mu_B                                                                                      \\
		\left(n_B \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \mu_i - n_A \sum_{i=n_A + 1}^n \mu_i\right)/\sqrt{n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2} \\
		\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \right)/\sqrt{n}
	\end{bmatrix}.
$$
\paragraph{Non-central Chi-square}
Let $X_1, \dots, X_m$ independent normally distributed random variables with means $a_1, \dots, a_m$ and unit variances and set $\xi = \sum_{i=1}^m a_i^2$ (non-centrality parameter). Then, $\sum_{i = 1}^m X_i^2$ follows a non-central chi-squared distribution with $m$ degrees of freedom and is denoted by $\chi^2_m(\xi)$.

In order to find the limit in \eqref{eq:Z_law} in terms of independent non-central chi-squares, we need to compute
$$
	\xi_A = \sum_{i=1}^{n_A -1} b_i^2, \quad \xi_B = \sum_{i=n_A}^{n-2} b_i^2, \text{ and } \quad \xi_{\text{neg}} = b_{n-1}^2.
$$
Denote by $\tilde P_{11}^T$ the orthogonal $n_A \times n_A$ matrix obtained by adding $(1/\sqrt{n_A}, \dots, 1/\sqrt{n_A}) \in \R^{n_A}$  as an additional row to $P_{11}^T$. We then get
\begin{align*}
	\xi_A  = \sum_{i=1}^{n_A -1} b_i^2 =
	  & \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \left(\left(\tilde P_{11}^T \mu_A \right)_i \right)^2 - |(1/\sqrt{n_A}, \dots, 1/\sqrt{n_A}) \mu_A|^2 \right) \\
	= & \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \mu_i^2 - \frac{1}{n_A} \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \mu_i \right)^2 \right),
\end{align*}
where we have used the orthogonality of $\tilde P_{11}$ in the last equality. Similarly, one gets
$$
	\xi_B = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \left(\sum_{i=n_A}^{n-2} \mu_i^2 - \frac{1}{n_B} \left(\sum_{i=n_A}^{n-2} \mu_i \right)^2 \right)
$$
and
$$
	\xi_{\text{neg}} = \frac{\left(n_B \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \mu_i - n_A \sum_{i=n_A + 1}^n \mu_i \right)^2}{\sigma^2\left(n_A^2 n_B + n_A n_B^2\right)}.
$$
Altogether, we can rewrite \eqref{eq:Z_law} to get
\begin{align}\label{eq:Z_law2}
	Z \eqdis
	 & \frac{\sigma^2}{n-1} \left(
	\frac{2 n_B}{n_A -1} \chi^2_{n_A -1}(\xi_A)
	+ \frac{2 n_A}{n_B -1} \tilde \chi^2_{n_B -1}(\xi_B)
	- (n+1) \bar \chi_1^2(\xi_{\text{neg}})
	\right),
\end{align}
where $\chi^2_{n_A -1}(\xi_A), \tilde \chi^2_{n_B -1}(\xi_B)$ and $\bar \chi_1^2(\xi_{\text{neg}})$ are independent.
In the special case where $\mu_A = (c, \dots, c)^T$ is constant, we get that $\xi_A = 0$ (same for $\mu_B$). If $\mu = (c, \dots, c)^T$, then $\xi_{\text{neg}} = \xi_A = \xi_B = 0$, i.e. we get central chi squared distributions.


\paragraph{Hypothesis test}
In practice, one would check the criterion we have mentioned above and decide if
a split is good or bad. That is compare the $ MSEP_split $ to $ MSEP $ However,
one should also account for randomness. Naturally, we
should not accept the criterion based on the outcome of the criterion. Instead,
in some sense, we would like to have some certainty or confidence in the
outcome. Say, we want to be $ 95\% $ certain that the outcome of $ Z $ is not
subject to a lot of noise. This naturally leads to hypothesis testing. We state a null-hypothesis as
following:
\begin{align*}
	H_0: & \mu_A = \mu_B = \mu.
\end{align*}
The null-hypothesis states that there is no difference in the mean function in
the two seperate regions. We then get that the distriubtion of $ Z $ under $ H_0
$ call it $ Z_0 $ is distributed as
\begin{align*}
	Z \eqdis & \frac{\sigma^2}{n-1} \left(
	\frac{2 n_B}{n_A -1} \chi^2_{n_A -1}
	+ \frac{2 n_A}{n_B -1} \tilde \chi^2_{n_B -1}
	- (n+1) \bar \chi_1^2
	\right).
\end{align*}
In fact, given the null-hyptohesis $ H_0 $, the individual Chi-Squared random variables are
centered as well. If the null-hypothesis is true we would expect the outcome of $ Z $ to be
somewhere around $ 0 $. In contrast, if $ H_0 $ is untrue, then, we expect the
outcome of $ Z $ to be very negative. If the out come of $ Z $ is realised in the $
	p\%$ quantile of $ Z_0 $, we reject $ H_0 $ since the outcome is to extreme
under $ H_0 $. Can we find an analytic quantile?

\subsection{Ratio distribution}
Let $H_0 : \mu = (c, \dots, c)^T$ for some $c \in \R$ and assume for the following that $H_0$ holds true. In order to find a statistic which does not depend on the unknown parameter $\sigma^2$, we would like to investigate the distribution of the ratio
$$
	R = \frac{
		\frac{n_A + 1}{n_A - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} (Y_i - \bar Y_A)^2 + \frac{n_B + 1}{n_B - 1} \sum_{i= n_A + 1}^{n} (Y_i - \bar Y_B)^2
	}{
		\frac{n+1}{n -1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (Y_i - \bar Y)^2
	}.
$$
For this purpose, we note that for each $r > 0$,
\begin{align*}
	\P(R > r) = \P(Z_r > 0),
\end{align*}
where
$$
	Z_r = \frac{n_A + 1}{n_A - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} (Y_i - \bar Y_A)^2 + \frac{n_B + 1}{n_B - 1} \sum_{i= n_A + 1}^{n} (Y_i - \bar Y_B)^2 - r

$$

$$
